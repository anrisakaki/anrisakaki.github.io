<details>

War results in demographic shocks and sex imbalances, both of which have been shown to increase female labour force participation (FLFP). Existing evidence on the nexus between conflict and FLFP predominantly stems from developed nations, which may not fully capture dynamics in developing contexts. This paper examines the impact of the Vietnam War on Vietnamese womenâ€™s labour market outcomes 14 to 44 years after its conclusion. Using comprehensive historical data on ordnance deployed by the United States in Vietnam matched to microdata, I find that while socialist ideology may have contributed to high FLFP rates in North Vietnam, war-induced demographic shocks contributed to increased FLFP rates in South Vietnam. A one-standard-deviation increase in historical bombing exposure is associated with a 1.03 to 1.57 percentage-point higher probability of Southern women working. In contrast, exposure to the Vietnam War is not associated with FLFP outcomes in the North. Evidence suggests that war widows increased their labour supply to compensate for income loss due to the death of their husbands, and daughters of widows are more likely to work than daughters of non-widows. However, I find limited support for demand-side mechanisms such as substitution toward female labour.
